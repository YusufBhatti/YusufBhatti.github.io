Yusuf Bhatti - Model Plots

Master of Research Student in Climate and Atmospheric Science

For the computer project, I have produced climate model plots [below] which have been run on the UM-UKCA (more details can be seen in the 'Scripts' or 'Research' section). The following model plots are visualising different magnitude eruptions from Mt Agung, releasing 10 and 20 Tg SO2 into the stratosphere (@ 21-23 km altitude). AOD stands for Aerosol Optical Depth, which measures how much visible and infrared light is reflected and absorbed by the atmosphere due to the presence of aerosols. Aerosol particles can include: windblown dust, volcanic ash/aerosol, wildfire smoke and sea salt. The focus of this research is solely volcanic aerosol. When the AOD is less than 0.1, a maximum visibility with clear skys are present. If the AOD is ~1, the conditions can be very hazy.

The monthly data set takes the monthly mean value, the daily data set takes the daily mean. Therefore, the daily measurements demonstrate a much greater variation in global distribution. The optic depth is at the wavelength 550 nm for the initial 13 months after the eruption.

Monthly Radiative Forcing Effects @ 10 Tg SO2

Figure 5. UM-UKCA monthly radiative forcing effects from the volcanic eruption (10 Tg SO2-emissions Agung-free-running). The effects were calculated from subtracting the total top of the atmosphere outgoing radiative flux from the perturbed stratosphere from ‘normal’ conditions (non-perturbed). GMOR = Global Mean Outgoing Radiation. For a comparison, Mt Pinatubo injected 14-20 Tg SO2 and led to a 3-month mean radiative forcing peak of -3 W/m2 and Tambora had a radiative forcing peak of 6.1 W/m2 (Self et al., 2004; Schmidt et al., 2018).
